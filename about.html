Lorraine Hall Partridge

I'm a junior developer at the Mississippi Coding Academy in Jackson, Mississippi, on the next part of my life's path , making a full circle from my beginnings as a NASA intern in the Data Processing Center of the Johnson Space Center. On the way, I've been a roadie, worked in a truck stop, and learned French. I spent three decades changing the world one person at a time in the inner city, bringing the world to my students and empowering them to seek their own horizons.

I'm moving on now, empowering my clients to overcome the challenges on their own paths.

Contact me and we'll seek a unique solution to your challenge.

bio

home

My path began when my English nanny mother married my Southern father, giving me roots deep in Mississippi and England. An early reader frustrated at school, I became an autodidact, reading my way through the library to learn as much about the world as I could.

My relationship with code began with creating codes and ciphers for my own amusement in elementary school, creating a fluidity in my thinking that made math and language come easily to me. At the same time, my disillusionment with formal education led my path to the dark side. In high school, parties rather than classes became my norm. A true imposter, my top grades belied my misspent youth.

Growing up watching the Apollo missions inspired my dream to work for NASA. But then my father died, and there was no one left to guide my path . Even so, my dream of working at NASA came true, and I spent two semesters as a college intern in the Data Processing Center at the Johnson Space Center in Houston, Texas, doing data analysis with the Mitre Corporation, learning Database Management at Sperry Univac and coding in Fortran. Unfortunately, I could not overcome the personal challenges that I met, and my path wandered into a thicket for several years until I answered the call to teach.

After a few years of travel and adventures, I became interested in the education of gifted children. Dissatisfied with the status quo, I decided to teach to help solve the problem myself. I returned to school with the goal of teaching gifted children, initially earning the credentials to teach math and French. Although math was my first love, I found myself teaching French and loving it.

Growing up during the Civil Rights era in Mississippi made a huge influence on me, and I gradually realized that serving gifted children in the inner city was my calling. When I landed at Lanier High School, once the only secondary school for African Americans in Mississippi, I had no idea I would stay for three decades. It took a worldwide pandemic for my path to move on.

Now my path joins the information superhighway and I am once again writing code. My vision is to develop instructional materials for French teachers using interactive animation. I continue to follow the path before me, still the intrepid traveller of my youth. Join me on my way to my next adventure!

The Crow and the Pitcher

In a spell of dry weather, when the Birds could find very little to drink, a thirsty Crow found a pitcher with a little water in it. But the pitcher was high and had a narrow neck, and no matter how he tried, the Crow could not reach the water. The poor thing felt as if he must die of thirst.

Then an idea came to him. Picking up some small pebbles, he dropped them into the pitcher one by one. With each pebble the water rose a little higher until at last it was near enough so he could drink.
